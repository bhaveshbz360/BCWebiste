Do You Really Need to Integrate SharePoint with Salesforce?

Although Salesforce development services let you upload and manage documents, they also have limitations. 

To prevent these limitations from affecting productivity, companies often turn to document collaboration systems like SharePoint to store documents and facilitate collaborations across the Salesforce integration company.

But is it really important to integrate these two systems? Let’s see what happens if you stick solely to using Salesforce for your processes.

  • File size: In Salesforce, you can upload documents using two options: “Attachments,” which supports files up to 25 MB, and “File,” which allows files up to 2 GB. However, managing data larger than this can lead to higher storage costs and slow down system performance over time due to the platform’s multitenant architecture.
  • Real-time syncing: Changes made on users’ local drives don’t automatically sync with Salesforce files, and vice versa. Users must upload or download files and then replace them with the new version, as no real-time syncing option is available.
  • Document accessibility and privacy: Salesforce documents can only be accessed by non-Salesforce users through a public URL, which raises concerns about GDPR compliance and other data security laws due to the public sharing of documents. One solution is to purchase additional licenses for non-Salesforce users, which can be costly.

In another scenario, businesses might use Salesforce and SharePoint separately without integrating them. This approach has problems.

  • Inefficient process: Contracts for vendors, customers, and partners often follow the same approval process, which is usually automated within a single workflow management system like SharePoint. However, when it comes to approving contracts, Salesforce users must switch between CRM and SharePoint and vice versa.
  • Document duplication: It is another issue when Salesforce isn’t integrated with SharePoint. Typically, the sales team needs to download documents from SharePoint and then upload them to Salesforce whenever they need to share them with their team or clients. This often results in having the same document stored in two systems, causing confusion with file versions.
  • Glitchy team collaboration: Team collaboration becomes challenging when customer records are stored in Salesforce, but collaboration occurs across both SharePoint and Salesforce. This leads to Salesforce users spending time switching between systems to stay connected with their teams, causing inefficiencies.
  • Content desertion: Content abandonment occurs when sales teams copy links to SharePoint files in Salesforce but cannot preview or locate them using Salesforce’s global search. Consequently, valuable content for customer communication ends up buried within SharePoint libraries.

Now that we’ve explored the drawbacks of not integrating SharePoint and Salesforce, let’s understand the clear benefits of establishing integration before we proceed to the integration steps.

Integrating SharePoint with Salesforce makes analytical and reporting tasks simpler. Step-by-Step Guide to Integrating SharePoint with Salesforce

When it comes to the process of integrating Salesforce and Sharepoint, you’ll generally have three options: Using Files Connect, custom tools for Salesforce Sharepoint integration, or utilizing third-party platforms to link the two systems. Let’s get into the details of these options.

Method 1: Using the Salesforce Files Connect SharePoint Method

  • First, go to the Salesforce AppExchange and search for “Files Connect.” Then, click on the “Get It Now” button to start the installation process.
  • After installing, navigate to the “Permission Sets” section in the Salesforce setup menu. Select “Files Connect” and assign appropriate user permissions, such as viewing, editing, and deleting files.
  • Then, set up an authentication provider by going to “Authentication Provider” and choosing “Files Connect.” Here, you can create and manage necessary settings, such as authentication type and users granted access.
  • Next, configure the SharePoint account by navigating to the “SharePoint” section and choosing “Files Connect.” Here, you can input the required information, such as login credentials and SharePoint URL.
  • Now, it’s time to configure SharePoint application development services. Navigate to the “Develop” section of the Salesforce setup menu and choose “Files Connect.” Here, you can configure the necessary settings, such as the type of integration and the data that will be shared between the systems.
  • The next important step for integrating Salesforce with SharePoint is to set up the Salesforce Files Connect tool. Go to the “Setup” section in the Salesforce menu and select “Files Connect.” Here, you can configure settings such as the integration type and the data that will be shared between the systems.
  • The final step of integrating Salesforce with SharePoint is to create a connection between the two platforms. To do this, go to the “External Data Sources” section in the Salesforce setup menu and select “Files Connect.” Here, you can configure settings such as the integration type and the data that will be shared between the systems.

While this method is commonly used for integrating Salesforce and SharePoint, there are some concerning limitations with Files Connect. 

These include limited support for external systems, lack of upload options, absence of permission controls, complexity in external sharing, latency issues, and search limitations.

Most Important Data Business Analytics Techniques

Using diverse data business analytics techniques can help you gain competitive benefits. Explore these data analytics techniques to choose the most suitable based on your business requirements.

1. Regression Analysis 

Regression analysis is a series of statistical methods that assist in evaluating the connection between dependent and independent variables.

Data scientists use regression analysis to assess the alliance of the variables and to simulate the future relationship between them. 

Additionally, it can also be used to check the independent variables’ impact on dependent variables. 

Businesses can also identify the connection between various volumes of product sales, and their pricing.

There are various types of Regression Analysis, like:

  1. Linear Regression: This analysis is a statistical method that finds the relationship between two variables by aligning a straight line to the data points.
  2. Ridge Regression: This technique is used to avoid overfitting in linear regression models by adding a penalty term to the coefficients.
  3. Logistic Regression: It is also an innovative statistical analysis in business. This method is used as predictive analytics for businesses to estimate the probability of a binary outcome depending on one or more predictor variables.
  4. Polynomial Regression: It is a type of regression analysis where the connection between the dependent and independent variable is modeled as an nth-degree polynomial.
  5. Lasso Regression: This is a method that performs both variable selection and regularization to interpret the statistical model and enhance the prediction accuracy.

Some of the major applications of Regression Analysis are:

  1. Sales and Promotions Forecasting
  2. Financial Data Analytics Forecasting (such as House Price Estimates or Stock Prices)
  3. Testing Automobiles
  4. Time Series Forecasting
  5. Weather Analysis and Prediction

2. Cluster Analysis 

Cluster analysis is another data business analytics technique used to identify general patterns and explain data. 

It is utilized when the data lacks clear labels and has complex labeling. The steps include identifying similar observations and assembling them to build clusters, and at the end assigning names and categories to these groups.

Cluster analysis helps to identify similarities and differences within datasets and showcase these to facilitate visual comparisons easily. In this approach, box plot visualizations are used frequently to highlight data clusters.

There are various types of Cluster Analysis, like:

  1. Connectivity-based Clustering (Hierarchical clustering): It groups data points based on their proximity to each other, forming hierarchical clusters that can be seen in a tree-like structure.
  2. Distribution-based Clustering: This identifies clusters by modeling the distribution of data points using statistical techniques like Gaussian mixture models.
  3. Centroids-based Clustering (Partitioning methods): It assigns data points to clusters by calculating the distance between each point and the centroid of a cluster, with popular methods including K-means and K-medoids.
  4. Density-based Clustering (Model-based methods): This forms clusters based on regions of high data point density, effectively identifying clusters of varying shapes and sizes in the data.
  5. Constraint-based (Supervised Clustering): This clustering incorporates prior knowledge or constraints into the clustering process, guiding the formation of clusters based on specific criteria or rules.
  6. Fuzzy Clustering: This allows data points to belong to multiple clusters with diverse membership degrees, accommodating uncertainty and overlapping clusters.

Some of the major applications of Cluster Analysis are:

  1. Geographic Information Systems (GIS)
  2. Collaborative Filtering
  3. Biology and Genetics
  4. Image Segmentation
  5. Document Clustering

3. Factor Analysis

Factor analysis is also a type of statistical technique. It is used to decrease the large numerical variables to a small number of factors by exporting common variance. This is referred as dimension or data reduction.

It works in a manner where they exhibit correlations among themselves when multiple separate or observable variables are connected with a shared concept.

Factor analysis is the most trusted approach when it comes to examining variable correlations for complex subjects like psychological dimensions and socioeconomic status.

These business analytics techniques can help in examining if there’s any connection between the group of factors or not. The initial variable relationship structure can be examined by additional variables or elements that are disclosed at the end of this process.

There are various types of Factor Analysis, like:

  1. Principal Component Analysis: This is a statistical business analytics technique used to simplify and identify patterns in data by changing it into a set of linearly uncorrelated variables called principal components.
  2. Image Factor Analysis: This method analyzes the common factors underlying a set of images, attempting to identify shared features or patterns among them.
  3. Common Factor Analysis: It is a statistical method used to determine the underlying structure of observed variables by identifying common factors that explain their relationship.
  4. Maximum Likelihood Approach: This method is used to predict the parameters of a factor model by increasing the likelihood function, letting the identification of factors that best explain the observed data.

Some of the major applications of Factor Analysis are:

  1. Marketing
  2. Machine Learning
  3. Nutritional Science
  4. Data Mining Techniques for Companies

4. Time Series Analysis

Time series analysis is a statistical data business analytics technique that recognizes patterns, trends, and cycles over time.

Time series data like weekly sales, monthly email sign-ups, and more are a set of data points that measure the same variable multiple times. In such cases, the data analysts can predict the future variations based on the variable interest by analyzing trends related to time.

This analysis requires a huge data to maintain reliability and consistency. In simple words, time series is a collection of data points arranged in sequence and the analysis is the act of interpreting that data.

There are various types of Time Series Analysis, like:

  1. Descriptive Analysis: This examines historical data to summarize and describe key characteristics and trends over time.
  2. Explanative Analysis: This analysis seeks to identify and understand the factors that influence changes in a time series, exploring relationships between variables to provide explanations for observed patterns.
  3. Exploratory Analysis: This involves examining a time series dataset to discover hidden patterns, relationships, or anomalies, often using visualizations and statistical techniques to generate hypotheses for further investigation.
  4. Intervention Analysis: This evaluates the impact of external events or interventions on a time series, such as policy changes or marketing campaigns, to assess their effectiveness and understand their effects on the data.
  5. Curve Fitting: It involves fitting mathematical models or equations to a time series dataset to describe its underlying trend or pattern, enabling better understanding and prediction of future values.
  6. Segmentation: This analysis divides a time series dataset into distinct segments or clusters based on similarities in patterns or characteristics, allowing for targeted analysis and decision-making for different subsets of the data.

Some of the major applications of Time Series Analysis are:

  1. Economic Forecasting
  2. Energy Consumption Forecasting
  3. Stock Market Analysis
  4. Quality Control
  5. Traffic and Transportation

5. Monte Carlo Simulation

The Monte Carlo is a business analytics technique used to predict the possible outcome of an unpredictable event. These estimates are made based on range of values rather than fixed set of values.

Computers are used for Monte Carlo simulation to examine data and predict outcomes about the course of action. 

This data analytics technique is also used to identify the impact of uncertainty and risk.

The Monte Carlo technique is also known as a multiple probability simulation or Monte Carlo method.

There are various types of Monte Carlo Simulation, like:

  1. Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC): MCMC is a computational method used to estimate the distribution of complex systems by systematically sampling from a Markov chain until it converges to the desired distribution.
  2. Rare Event Simulation: It is a technique used to estimate the probability of rare or extreme events occurring in a time series by simulating the system over a large number of iterations.
  3. Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): This algorithm is used to find optimal decisions in decision trees or game trees by simulating future states and selecting the most promising actions based on the outcomes of these simulations.
  4. Multi-Level Monte Carlo (MLMC): MLMC is a method that combines multiple levels of approximation to efficiently estimate expectations or probabilities in time series, reducing computational cost while maintaining accuracy.
  5. Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS): LHS is a technique used to generate samples from a multidimensional distribution in a way that ensures uniform coverage of the sample space, often employed in time series analysis to reduce sampling bias and improve efficiency.

Some of the major applications of Monte Carlo Simulation are:

  1. Structural Analysis
  2. Reliability Analysis
  3. Optimization Problems
  4. Queueing Systems
  5. Climate Modeling
  6. Natural Disaster Risk Assessment

6. Cohort Analysis

Cohort analysis is a popular data business analytics technique that breaks or bifurcates common characteristics data into groups before the analysis. 

This technique is used to better understand user behavior in particular cohorts, optimize customer retention, and make it easier for organizations to analyze, isolate, and, uncover trends in the user lifetime.

There are various types of Cohort Analysis, like:

  1. Time-Based Cohort Analysis: This analysis groups customers based on their first interaction or purchase date to track their behavior and performance over time, providing insights into changes in customer engagement and retention rates.
  2. Demographic Cohort Analysis: It categorizes customers into groups based on demographic attributes like age, gender, or location, allowing businesses to understand how different demographic segments behave and interact with their products or services.
  3. Acquisition Cohort Analysis: This analysis groups customers based on the time they were acquired or joined the business, enabling companies to evaluate the effectiveness of their marketing and acquisition strategies over time.
  4. Monetary Cohort Analysis: It segments customers based on their spending behavior or purchase amounts, helping businesses identify high-value customers, analyze spending trends, and tailor marketing efforts accordingly.
  5. Retention Cohort Analysis: This analysis categorizes customers based on their retention or repeat purchase behavior, allowing companies to identify factors influencing customer churn, assess customer loyalty, and implement strategies to improve retention rates over time.

Some of the major applications of Cohort Analysis are:

  1. Healthcare Data Analytics and Patient Outcomes
  2. Product and Feature Performance
  3. Subscription and Membership Services
  4. Social Sciences
  5. Real Estate

Mistakes in Data Science Talent Acquisition

Let’s look at some of the most common mistakes recruiters often make when hiring data scientists for their business. 

Error #1: Lack of Clarity in Job Descriptions

When recruiting data science talent, the mistakes are often in the details, and it starts with the job description.

Crafting a clear and comprehensive job description is a critical step that many organizations tend to overlook. 

The consequences of a vague or confusing job posting can be significant, leading to a mismatch of expectations and a waste of time for both the employer and the candidate.

Let’s delve into this crucial pain point and explore why lack of clarity in job descriptions is a common mistake:

Why it matters? 

  • Attracting the Right Candidates: Ambiguous job descriptions can attract a flood of resumes, but they may not be from candidates with the skills you need.
  • Wasting Time and Resources: Sorting through countless applications from ill-fitting candidates takes time and effort.
  • Mismatched Expectations: A lack of clarity can result in candidates expecting something different from the role, leading to frustration and potential turnover.

Whether you hire Machine Learning engineers or data scientists, recruitment teams must ensure a clear job description to reach their potential candidates.

Error #2: Overemphasizing Academic Credentials in Recruiting Data Science Talent

When recruiting data science talent, it’s easy to get caught up in the allure of academic degrees.

While a solid educational background is undoubtedly necessary, you must pay more attention to academic credentials to ensure your ability to find the right talent for your IT projects. 

Let’s break down why this happens and what you should keep in mind:

  • Degrees Don’t Always Reflect Practical Skills: Academic degrees provide a foundation of knowledge but may only sometimes align with practical skills needed in the IT industry. A candidate with many degrees might need more hands-on experience.
  • The Data Science Field Is Evolving Rapidly: Data science is dynamic, with new tools and techniques emerging regularly. Relying solely on academic qualifications might mean missing out on candidates who stay current with the latest advancements.
  • Diverse Backgrounds Bring Valuable Perspectives: Overemphasizing degrees might lead to overlooking candidates from diverse backgrounds. Data science benefits from multidisciplinary insights, and candidates with non-traditional backgrounds can offer fresh perspectives.
  • Practical Experience Matters: Instead of fixating on degrees, consider a candidate’s practical experience. Look for projects they’ve worked on, contributions to open-source initiatives, or their ability to solve real-world data problems.

While academic credentials hold importance in the recruitment process, they shouldn’t be the sole focus. 

To recruit the best data science talent for your IT projects, consider a candidate’s practical skills, experience, problem-solving abilities, creativity, and communication skills.

Error #3: Ignoring Soft Skills in Recruiting Data Science Talent

When we talk about recruiting data science talent, it’s not all about numbers, algorithms, and coding. 

While technical expertise is essential, ignoring soft skills in hiring can be a major pitfall. This is one of the common things that could be improved in hiring data scientists that recruiters often make. 

Here’s why soft skills matter and how they can make a significant difference in finding the right fit for your IT projects:

  •  Effective Communication: Data scientists work in collaboration with cross-functional teams. Strong communication skills are crucial for explaining complex findings to non-technical stakeholders, making your data-driven insights actionable.
  • Teamwork and Collaboration:  IT projects often involve multifaceted teams. Data scientists must work effectively with engineers, designers, and business analysts. Ignoring teamwork skills can lead to project bottlenecks and misalignment.
  • Problem-Solving and Critical Thinking: Data science is all about solving problems. Soft skills like critical thinking and analytical reasoning enable data scientists to approach challenges creatively and find innovative solutions.
  • Adaptability: The tech landscape evolves rapidly. Data scientists who are adaptable and open to learning new tools and techniques will thrive in an ever-changing environment.
  • Time Management: Meeting project deadlines is vital. Data scientists who excel in time management ensure that data analyses are completed on schedule, preventing project delays.
  • Emotional Intelligence: Soft skills like empathy and emotional intelligence enhance collaboration and communication, making it easier to navigate team dynamics. Whether you hire an AI engineer or a software engineer, soft skills are as important as tech skills.

Error #4: Neglecting Technical Assessments in Recruiting Data Science Talent

Recruiting data science talent is more than just a resume-scanning exercise. One challenge in hiring data science professionals is evaluating beyond academic skills. 

While resumes may list impressive qualifications, neglecting technical assessments can be a critical misstep in finding the right fit for your IT projects. 

Here’s why technical assessments matter and how they can help you avoid data science staffing blunders:

  • Uncovering Practical Skills: Resumes may showcase academic achievements but sometimes reveal a candidate’s practical skills. Technical assessments provide a hands-on way to gauge candidates’ ability to apply their knowledge.
  • Assessing Problem-Solving Abilities: Data science is all about solving complex problems. Technical assessments present candidates with real-world scenarios, allowing you to determine their problem-solving and critical-thinking skills.
  • Evaluating Tool Proficiency: Data scientists work with many tools and technologies. Technical assessments help you verify a candidate’s proficiency with specific tools crucial for your IT projects.
  •  Ensuring Alignment with Data Analytics: For roles specific to data analytics, such as recruiting missteps in data analytics, technical assessments can ensure that candidates possess the skills needed to excel in this specialized area of data science.
  • Avoiding Overconfidence: Some candidates may excel in interviews but might not have impressive performance when asked to deal with practical problems. Technical assessments objectively measure a candidate’s abilities, preventing overconfidence biases.
  • Identifying Candidates Who ‘Fit’: Beyond technical skills, technical assessments can reveal a candidate’s approach to problem-solving, attention to detail, and work ethic, helping you find candidates who align.
  • Leveraging Expert AI Consulting Services: To ensure that technical assessments are rigorous and relevant, consider partnering with expert AI consulting services. They can help you design assessments that accurately reflect the skills required for your data science roles.

Error #5: Failing to Showcase Company Culture in Recruiting Data Science Talent

Recruiting data science talent isn’t just about finding the right skills; it’s also about finding the right fit for your company culture. 

Overlooking this critical aspect can lead to flawed data science recruitment strategies, errors in hiring machine learning experts, and a misalignment between your new hires and your organization’s values. 

Here’s why showcasing company culture matters:

  • Cultural Fit Matters:  A candidate might have all the technical skills you need, but if they don’t align with your company’s culture, it can lead to discord and a lack of synergy within your team.
  • Motivation and Engagement: Employees who resonate with your company culture are more likely to be motivated and engaged, contributing positively to your data science projects and overall business goals.
  • Retention and Loyalty: When data scientists feel a sense of belonging and share your company’s values, they are more likely to stay with your organization long-term, reducing turnover costs.
  • Collaborative Environment: Data science often involves collaboration between teams. Hiring individuals who embrace your company culture fosters a cooperative and harmonious work environment.
  • Brand Image: A strong company culture can enhance your brand image, making you more appealing to top data science talent in the competitive job market.
  • Tailored Recruitment Strategies:  When showcasing your company culture, tailor your recruitment strategies to highlight the unique aspects of your organization, whether it’s a focus on innovation, inclusivity, or a commitment to excellence.
  • Business Intelligence Consulting Services: To effectively showcase your company culture, consider leveraging Business Intelligence Consulting Services. They can help you analyze your culture and design strategies to attract candidates who resonate with it.
  • Culture Beyond the Buzzwords: It’s not enough to simply mention buzzwords like “innovative” or “collaborative” in your job postings. Provide concrete examples of how your company lives these values.

Error #6: Rushing the Hiring Process in Recruiting Data Science Talent

One of the common talent acquisition errors in data engineering is rushing the hiring process. This can lead to critical data science hiring pitfalls. 

Let’s explore why taking your time is essential:

  1. Finding the Right Fit Takes Time: Rushing through interviews and assessments can result in hiring candidates who may not be the best fit for your organization or data science projects.
  2. Evaluating Skills Thoroughly: Data science roles require a precise skill set. Taking your time allows you to thoroughly evaluate a candidate’s technical abilities, ensuring they meet the demands of your dedicated offshore development center or in-house team.
  3. Avoiding Misalignment: Hurried hiring can lead to a mismatch between a candidate’s expectations and the actual role, resulting in dissatisfaction and potential turnover.
  4. Cultural Fit Assessment: Assessing cultural fit is crucial. A rushed process may neglect to evaluate how well a candidate aligns with your organization’s values and work culture.
  5. Comprehensive Interviews: Interviews should go beyond technical questions. Taking time allows you to delve into a candidate’s problem-solving skills, communication abilities, and adaptability.
  6. Due Diligence: Data scientists often handle sensitive information. A rushed hiring process may skip necessary background checks, risking your data security.
  7. Dedicated Offshore Development Center Considerations: If you’re setting up a Dedicated Offshore Development Center, thorough hiring is vital to ensure a productive and successful offshore team.

Error #7: Disregarding Diversity and Inclusion

In the race to secure top data science talent, one critical aspect that often gets overlooked is diversity and inclusion. 

Considering diversity and inclusion can help you build an ideal team, as it can share diverse perspectives and capabilities. Here’s why diversity and inclusion matter:

  1. A Broader Range of Perspectives: Diverse teams bring together individuals from varied backgrounds and experiences. This diversity can lead to more innovative problem-solving and creative solutions.
  2. Representation Matters: Inclusivity conveys to underrepresented groups that they are valued and welcome in your organization. This can be a powerful driver for attracting diverse talent.
  3. Enhanced Creativity: Diverse teams tend to be more creative and can offer unique insights that a homogenous group might miss.
  4. Meeting Market Demands: In today’s global market, having a diverse team can help you better understand and meet the needs of a diverse customer base.
  5. Broadening Your Talent Pool: Rushing the hiring process might cause you to overlook qualified candidates from different backgrounds who could be a great fit for your team.
  6. Hire Remote Expert Data Scientists with Inclusivity in Mind: If you’re considering hiring remote expert data scientists, make sure your hiring practices are inclusive and welcoming to individuals from various locations and cultures.
  7. Dedicated Offshore Development Center and Diversity: When setting up a Dedicated Offshore Development Center, consider how diversity and inclusion can enhance the effectiveness of your offshore team.
  8. Creating a Welcoming Environment: Diversity and inclusion extend beyond the hiring process. Ensure your workplace culture is inclusive and fosters a sense of belonging for all team members.

Prioritizing diversity and inclusion in your hiring process can lead to a more vibrant, dynamic, and successful data science team.
